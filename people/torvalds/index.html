Linus B. Torvalds

Linus Benedict Torvalds is a Finnish software developer known for developing the Linux kernel, the most popular UNIX-based kernel used in GNU/Linux, Android and Chrome OS. He also developed the Git version control system, increasingly popular amongst smaller software developers and used in the development of the Linux kernel.

After his mandatory stint in the Finnish Army, Linus attended the University of Helsinki in 1988. There, he read Andrew Tannenbaum's Operating Systems: Design and Implementation, where he developed his love affair with UNIX. He titled his Master's thesis Linux: A Portable Operating System.

Ever since he was 11 years old, Linus programmed for the Commodore VIC-20, eventually writing his own assembler and Pac-Man clone. He recieved his first 80386 IBM PC clone in 1991, where he also got introduced to Richard Stallman and the GNU Project. He released the first version of Linux later that year, and 1.0 in 1994.

He worked at Transmeta from 1997 until 2003, where he moved on to the Open Source Development Labs. The OSDL merged with the Free Standards Group to become the Linux Foundation, who still employs him to be head maintainer of the Linux kernel. His role is mostly merging code written by millions of other individuals and corporate entities, e.g. Red Hat and Google.
