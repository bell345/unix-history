Richard M. Stallman

Richard Matthew Stallman is a free software activist and computer programmer most well known for founding the GNU Project and the Free Software Foundation, efforts that promote the idea of copyleft; that uses copyright law to ensure that users are given many rights to software without them being taken away.

Stallman was inspired to vehemently pursue free software after a series of copyright issues with one of them being unable to modify the software on a Xerox 9700 to notify the campus of print jobs. In September of 1983, Stallman announced to the world his GNU Project, with the end goal of creating a completely free and open-source operating system based upon UNIX.

The GNU Project had almost all the parts of a fully-functioning operating system, but still lacked a decent kernel. Linus Torvalds created his own kernel using GNU tools, and soon it became the leading avenue for the GNU Project's software. Stallman prefers to call this system GNU/Linux, as he feels the short name, Linux, undermines the GNU Project and its work.

Stallman is extremely privacy concerned, and often takes and recommends drastic measures to protect it. For example, he doesn't own a mobile phone, as it enables easy government/malicious tracking, and he also doesn't fetch websites from his web browser: he has a remote server fetch them and send them to him securely.
