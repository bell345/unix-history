Early Adoption of UNIX

The first users of the growing UNIX system was Bell Labs' internal departments on DEC computers, winning out over DEC's own operating system. Porting to other computers such as the Interdata 8/32, the UNIVAC 1100 and (later) an Intel 8086-based computer was much easier for UNIX since it was mostly written in C. Technically, any computer with a C compiler could run UNIX.

Adoption outside of the Labs was helped by an antitrust ruling in 1953 that stated that AT&T must never enter the computer business. That meant that UNIX had to be distributed to anybody who asked because they couldn't sell it. The PDPs and other minicomputers that UNIX ran on were far too expensive for regular users. There were no personal computers during the 1970s, mind you. But its prolific use in universities gave rise to the next generation of rebellious computer hackers — minicomputers were often not as regulated as the larger mainframes, and this led to a counterculture against IBM and corporate computing that persists to this day.

When Version 7 was released in 1979, UNIX had evolved from a small PDP-7 project to a complete system that spread to universities as far away as Australia (UNSW, to be precise). By the late 1970s, commercial UNIX developments started to take place — SCO was the first company that sold UNIX commercially. Even a small Seattle software company who had previously only made an Altair BASIC compiler began to sell a modified UNIX, rebranded as XENIX.
