UNIX Wars - Berkeley vs. AT&T

When AT&T moved into the UNIX market, two things were beginning to take shape. One, the first round of UNIX infighting, often referred to as the UNIX Wars, and two, Intel microprocessors (specifically the 386) started to become powerful enough to run UNIX on personal machines. This not only meant that UNIX could become even more widespread, but it also meant that workstation vendors, such as Sun and SCO had a much easier job of making the perfect UNIX workstation.

The fighting over System V (AT&T) and BSD stemmed from the different cultures of the two platforms. BSD hackers were more free and anti-authority than those working on SysV, which they saw as backwards and bogged down by corporate mess. There were also technical disputes; over the networking APIs (streams vs. sockets) for example.

Efforts to unify the competing UNIX standards began in earnest with the 1985 POSIX standards, which made executive decisions on the functionality of a standardised UNIX. For example, Berkeley sockets were preferred over SysV streams, but SysV terminal controls were preferred over BSD tty. All derived UNIXes coming after adhered to these standards to some extent, which helped to quell the current UNIX disputes.

In 1985, Richard Stallman founded the GNU Project, with an end goal of creating a fully open and free operating system from the ground-up. And in the beginning, although many people shared some of his ideals, he was never taken too seriously around the likes of IBM, Sun and AT&T, who were the epitome of proprietary commercial giants and who ruled the computer market.

Speaking of IBM, in 1986, they made a fatal decision that caused them to back out of the PC market. OS/2 and the machines it ran on, while gaining a foothold in the point-of-sale market, never took off in the personal computer market, partly due to the fact that it ran on older 286 processors instead of the newer 386. Compaq managed to outmode them and provide the first UNIX-capable personal computer with a 386, despite the 16 MHz clock speed.

Make no mistake, IBM's previous standard, the soon-to-be-ubiquitous IBM PC still posed a massive threat to the UNIX industry. Especially from Microsoft, which had grown from a Seattle podunk startup to a major player in the software market with their flagship, MS-DOS. The following decade revealed a new card up Microsoft's sleeves: Microsoft Windows 3.0, which made the GUI a standard interface and cemented their influence on the computing industry.
